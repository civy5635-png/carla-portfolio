    Character Animation

    My first Adobe Animate project was to create a short animation featuring a character performing a simple action.

    I didn't think it would take 6 hours to animate 4 seconds of video, but I was very much mistaken. My original idea was to use the squirrel character graphic to show a squirrel being chased by a dog, but I didn't like the dog graphic in the file, and I only had a few backgrounds in my Adobe Animate file. So I explored the backgrounds and downloaded several from stock.adobe.com. I couldn't find a dog graphic I liked and could use, so I pivoted and decided to stick with the characters already in my library, along with a downloaded bridge background. That was how I spent the first day.

    I decided to animate a squirrel on a bridge, heading toward a large acorn, just missing it as the macaw swoops in and flies away with it. Then the squirrel would leap up onto the railing, scurry toward the tree, go up it, and disappear. Later, I added a sound file of loud bird sounds to the animation. It started as 10 seconds, but I reduced it to 4 because the action happened fast, no matter how I tried to slow it down.

    The bridge background I pulled onto the stage came in 3 layer. I opened the file, and it was composed of multiple pieces of the animation in multiple files. I suspect this might be used to animate different parts of the background, but I decided that was beyond my current skill set, so, on the advice of ChatGPT, I clicked the bottom and top layers, selected all three, and converted it to a symbol. I then went back and reviewed the video and information on motion tween in the course in order to refresh my memory-then promptly locked it before I screwed it up. My next action was to pull the acorn graphic on the screen, use the free transform tool to scale it down to size, and the selection tool to position it on the bridge. Had to go back because I forgot to turn it into a symbol. I locked the layer and labeled each layer so I would not lose track of what was what. I then pulled the blue macaw on the stage and did the same. Consulting ChatGpt, I right-clicked it after I sized it and clicked 'transform', then horizontal flip' so it would be flying in the right direction. To show the macaw swooping down and picking up the acorn, I inserted keyframes at 43 and 53, then clicked Motion Tween to animate it. For some reason, it went to a classic tween instead of a motion tween, although I'm not sure why. Since the classic tween did what I wanted after 2 attempts to get it to look the way I wanted, I was fine with that. I would like to know exactly what I did that prevented it from going into motion tween. After getting the macaw to the acorn, I asked ChatGPT again how best to animate picking up the nut and flying off. This led to many attempts to cut/paste the acorn from the acorn layer onto the macaw layer. I couldn't make it work. I got frustrated and my butt hurt, so that was day 2.

    On day three, I started out with a different idea. I asked ChatGPT how to create a new macaw symbol with a nut in its beak on a different timeline, taking over from the pre-acorn-stealing macaw. I duplicated the macaw symbol, double-clicked it to isolate it, and pulled the acorn from the library onto the stage again. It didn't work the first time I tried this, but then I noticed the 'edit' option when I right-clicked the macaw. I don't remember anything about this when I attempted the cut/paste nut the day before, so this may be why it didn't work. In edit, I positioned the acorn at the macaw's beak, clicked Arrange > Send Backward on the nut, then I converted both to a single symbol. I don't recall ChatGPT telling me to put it in edit mode, but it might have assumed I'd know to do that. It's never a good idea to assume I know something computer-related, no matter how much of a 'duh' something it is. I pulled the new symbol, macaw_with_nut, onto the stage as its own layer. On frame 53, I lined up the pre-acorn macaw with the post-acorn macaw, then used a classic tween to have the macaw fly away with the acorn. On the nut layer, I just cut the timeline from there. With the macaw, I inserted blank keyframes to the end of the timeline at frame 120. I'm not sure which is better, or if one is better than the other, but I was mostly happy with how it looked. After locking everything, I brought the squirrel to the stage on its own layer. I sized and placed it on the far end of the bridge, and made several attempts to have it scampering around, moving toward the acorn once it was spotted. I kept looking jerky, and once the squirrel randomly disappeared, I deleted that layer and started over multiple times(I lost count). At some point, I decided Ctrl + Z was my favorite keyboard function. Had to walk away for a while before I started hating that squirrel with a passion.

    Day 4, I'm tackling the squirrel again. The motion didn't look right, and the loop included an animated nut that suddenly appeared, which was another problem. I asked ChatGPT how to make it hold still for a few frames after the macaw swoops down to pick up the acorn. In the process, I discovered the frame picker under the loop option in the properties panel and realized I could set the first and last frames, cutting out the frames with the animated nut in the squirrel loop. After finally setting the tween, almost getting it to the nut, I put the squirrel in one position in a single frame for eight frames, so it would sit still staring at the empty place where the acorn had been. As the squirrel got closer to the nut, I used the free transform tool to increase the size a little, but I don't see that much of a difference. I added a WAV file of bird sounds from freesound for the animation. I clicked the 'clip everything off of stage key up top, thinking it would keep only what was on the stage, but it didn't work after I clicked file>export>video/media. It showed the entire white stage, so I deleted it and changed the background to brown to match the stage. I centered it, then exported again as an MP4 file. It still wasn't centered, but it did look better. A random extra macaw appears at the top left of the stage, but I wasn't sure where it came from and couldn't delete it. I deleted and re-tweened that squirrel so many times that I had to stop and walk away. I believe this is the best I can do with my current skill set-unless I want to keep driving myself crazy. I'm not happy with it. I don't know what else I could have done to get it centered or have the background fill the whole movie. figuring I haven't written anything for a college class in 30 years, so I hope this is what you were looking for. I might have gotten a little long-winded. I was absorbed in doing this while I was figuring it out, and I mostly enjoyed the process. I just wish I were happier with the result.
